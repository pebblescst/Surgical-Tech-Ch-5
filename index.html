Core principle: Every patient is unique. A positive surgical outcome depends on patient-centered, outcome-oriented care — the team bases all assessments, planning, and interventions on the patient as an individual.
Patient-Centered vs. Outcome-Oriented Care
Patient-centered care
Treats the patient as a whole person — physical, psychological, social, and spiritual. Respects individual values, preferences, and needs. Involves the patient and family as partners in care decisions.
Outcome-oriented care
Care is planned and evaluated based on measurable patient outcomes. Goals are set, interventions are chosen to achieve those goals, and outcomes are assessed. Evidence-based and goal-driven.
In the OR, even brief encounters with the patient require compassion and respect. The patient is awake, afraid, and vulnerable during the pre-op phase — the ST's demeanor and words matter.
Spirituality in Patient Care
Spirituality defined: A sense of something more profound than humanity; awareness of an energy or power greater than humankind. Not necessarily the same as religion.

In the perioperative setting:
✦ Patients express faith through prayer and sacred rituals
✦ Faith can play a powerful role in recovery
✦ Religious items must be safeguarded and returned before discharge
✦ All OR staff should know how to direct families to prayer areas and contact the chaplaincy service
✦ Religious laws may affect procedures: abortion, blood products, diet restrictions, euthanasia
Key rule: Care that conflicts with the patient's religious affiliation is NOT performed. If a patient refuses blood products or specific procedures for religious reasons, this must be documented and respected.
Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs
Maslow's hierarchy is a motivational theory describing a five-level pyramid of human needs. Lower-level needs must be met before higher-level needs can be addressed. In surgical care, the patient's most basic physiological and safety needs take priority.
5
Self-Actualization
Achieving one's full potential; purpose; personal growth; meaning — highest level need
4
Esteem
Self-esteem, achievement, mastery, recognition, respect from others and for oneself; includes body image
3
Love & Belonging
Friendships, intimacy, family, sense of connection; family and community support; love relationships
2
Safety & Security
Personal security, employment, resources, health, property; freedom from fear, anxiety, and chaos; order and stability
1
Physiological Needs
Most basic and must be met first: nutrition, water, shelter, air/oxygen, rest/sleep, elimination, movement, freedom from pain
Physiological Needs (Level 1) in the OR
Nutrition and water Shelter / Warmth Air and oxygen Rest and sleep Elimination Movement Freedom from pain
In the OR, the team directly addresses these: warming blankets (temperature), oxygen therapy, pain management, NPO status for fluid balance, Foley catheters for elimination, and positioning for circulation.
Psychological & Social Domains (Levels 2–5)
Safety and security — Patient fears anesthesia, pain, the unknown. The team provides clear information, consistency, and a calm environment.
Love and belonging — Family presence pre-op, chaplain access, therapeutic communication. Isolation increases anxiety.
Family and community support — Key factor in recovery and coping; family is a partner in patient care.
Esteem — Respect modesty/privacy, use patient's preferred name, maintain dignity during prep and draping. Body image concerns are real.
Self-actualization — Less directly applicable in the OR; relevant in long-term recovery and adjustment to surgical outcomes.
Common Preoperative Patient Fears
💉
Anesthesia (especially anesthesia awareness)
Fear of not waking up OR fear of being aware/conscious during surgery. Anesthesia awareness (accidental intraoperative consciousness) is rare but real — about 1-2 per 1,000 cases. Validate this fear; reassure that anesthesia providers monitor depth continuously.
💀
Death
Fear that surgery will lead to death; heightened in high-risk or emergency cases. Acknowledge openly if patient expresses this; do not minimize or dismiss. Never promise a specific outcome.
⚡
Pain
Fear of intraoperative and postoperative pain. Reassure that pain management is a priority before, during, and after surgery; patients are not left to suffer.
🪞
Disfigurement
Fear of visible scarring, loss of body parts, or changes to appearance. Common in oncologic, reconstructive, and orthopedic cases. Body image is tied to Maslow's esteem level.
🎮
Loss of control
Surgery requires the patient to relinquish control of their body and environment completely. This is profoundly anxiety-inducing. Providing information and choices (when possible) restores some sense of control.
👁️
Physical exposure
Fear of being undressed, examined, or observed in vulnerable positions. Maintain modesty with draping; only expose what is necessary; explain each step to the patient.
🔒
Loss of privacy
Fear that personal and medical information will be shared without consent. Reinforce HIPAA; explain who will be in the room; limit OR personnel to those necessary for the case.
Supportive Communication Techniques
Supportive listening
Give the patient your full attention. Maintain eye contact. Do not interrupt. Allow silences. Acknowledge what they say ("I hear you," "That sounds frightening").
Provide information
Answer questions honestly and within your scope. Tell the patient what to expect (sounds, sensations, what will happen next). Informed patients are less anxious.
Give reassurance
Reassure the patient they are in capable hands; the team is prepared; their comfort and safety are priorities. Never make specific outcome promises.
Provide clarification
If the patient misunderstands something, gently correct it. Ensure they have accurate information without overstepping the surgeon's role in discussing the procedure.
What NOT to say:
✗ "Don't worry, everything will be fine" — makes a promise you can't keep
✗ "This is a simple surgery" — minimizes the patient's concern
✗ Anything that minimizes, rationalizes, or denies the patient's fear
✗ Never imply that surgery will cure the patient — that is the surgeon's role
✗ Never provide information to family/friends that should come from the care team
Kübler-Ross Model — Five Stages of Dying
Proposed by psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross in 1969. These stages describe the emotional journey of dying patients — and can also apply to any significant loss. Stages are not always sequential, and not everyone experiences all five.
Stage 1
Denial
The patient refuses to accept or believe the terminal diagnosis. "This can't be happening to me." Denial serves as a psychological buffer, protecting the patient from an overwhelming reality. In the OR: patient may minimize their condition or express disbelief about their prognosis.
Stage 2
Anger
"Why me? It's not fair!" Anger may be directed at healthcare providers, family, God, or the situation. Do not take anger personally. Acknowledge the patient's feelings; avoid arguing or becoming defensive. The ST should maintain a calm, compassionate demeanor.
Stage 3
Bargaining
"If I can just live long enough to see my grandchildren..." The patient attempts to negotiate for more time, often with God, fate, or healthcare providers. May involve promises of changed behavior. Focus on listening; do not make bargains on behalf of the medical team.
Stage 4
Depression
A profound sadness and withdrawal as the reality of death sinks in. Patient may be quiet, tearful, or disengaged. Do not try to "cheer them up" or dismiss their sadness. Provide quiet, compassionate presence. This is a natural and necessary part of grieving.
Stage 5
Acceptance
"It's going to be okay." The patient reaches a peaceful understanding of their mortality. Not happiness — but a calm readiness. May focus on saying goodbyes, completing unfinished business. The patient is at peace; support this process with dignity and respect.
Applying Kübler-Ross in the OR: A terminally ill patient may be in any stage. The ST should: recognize the stage without judgment, respond with empathy, listen actively, never argue with denial or anger, and focus on immediate physical and emotional comfort. The ST does NOT provide information about prognosis to patient or family — that is the surgeon's and care team's role.
Death in the OR & Ethical Considerations
Death in the OR is rare, but every ST must understand the psychosocial and procedural aspects in case it occurs.
Perspectives on death in the clinical setting:
Social
Family and community impact; grief support needs; cultural practices for body preparation
Personal
Individual beliefs about death; personal history with loss affects the clinician's response
Ethical
Self-determination, right to die, DNR, palliative care, advance directives — all require ethical navigation
Legal
Determination of death; coroner involvement; cause of death documentation; legal implications of decisions
Medical
Clinical criteria for death; brain death protocols; postmortem care; organ procurement
Ethical considerations:
Self-determination: Individual's right to decide how to live or die — honored through advance directives
Right to die: Refusing treatment (legal) vs. assisted suicide (varies by state law)
Advance directives: DNR, organ donation, MPOA — must be in chart before OR proceeds
Palliative care: Makes the dying process less painful and more comfortable; does NOT provide a cure; improves quality of life
Natural Postmortem Changes
After death, predictable physical changes occur. The ST should be aware of these to handle the body with appropriate care and dignity.
Cooling (algor mortis)
Body temperature drops approximately 1–1.5°F per hour after death until it reaches ambient temperature
Loss of muscle tone
Muscles relax immediately after death; sphincters may release; body becomes limp
Eyes open, jaw drops
Facial muscles relax; eyes may remain partially open; jaw may fall open; handled with care during postmortem preparation
Livor mortis
Purplish-red discoloration of skin from pooling of blood in dependent areas; begins 1–2 hours after death; fixed after 6–12 hours
Rigor mortis
Stiffening of muscles from chemical changes in muscle fibers; begins 2–4 hours after death; complete within 12 hours; resolves within 48–72 hours
Postmortem care: The body is handled gently and with respect at all times. Care that conflicts with the patient's religious affiliation is NOT performed. Body preparation must be carried out with dignity.
Coroner's Cases — When Coroner Must Be Involved
Death in the OR or emergency department (ED)
Any death occurring in the OR or ED requires coroner notification regardless of expected cause
Unwitnessed death
No one was present to observe the death; circumstances must be investigated
Death after admission from another facility
Transfer death raises questions about cause and care; requires independent review
Criminal activity suspected
Any death where foul play, assault, or criminal negligence may have contributed
Suicide
All suicidal deaths require coroner investigation to confirm manner and cause
Death of an incarcerated individual
Prisoners who die require coroner review regardless of apparent natural cause
Death secondary to infectious disease
Public health implications require official notification and possible investigation
Cause of death is determined using the CDC International Classification of Diseases (ICD) coding system — required for all medical cases; influences family/community response and statistical data.
The Dying Patient's Family
Family's central role: Family has a central role in the patient's emotional environment; their support directly affects the patient's coping and recovery.

Family grief responses: Grief and sadness may accompany anger and frustration — often simultaneously. The family may need guidance to cope with the process.

Practical stressors: Financial burden previously borne by the patient may shift to other family members; this adds to emotional distress.

Sudden unexpected death: Requires cloistered privacy; family needs space away from other patients and visitors to grieve; chaplain and social work involvement recommended.
Critical ST rule: The surgical technologist should refrain from providing information to family or friends. All communication about the patient's condition, prognosis, or death must come from the surgeon or designated care team members.
Health professionals confronting death — factors that affect response: prior personal experience with death, support available in the workplace, personal beliefs and values, knowledge of the death process, and the professional's own emotional well-being.
Organ and Tissue Recovery
Organ donation saves lives. The ST may participate in organ procurement cases, which follow strict protocols and require the same level of care and dignity as any other surgical procedure.
Permission for recovery
Must be obtained from the patient (via advance directive) or legally designated next of kin/MPOA. Organ procurement organizations (OPOs) coordinate consent and recovery. Hospitals are required to notify OPOs of potential donors.
Protocols
Specific organ procurement protocols are followed for each organ type. Brain death must be declared by two independent physicians before procurement. All steps are documented carefully. The body is treated with the same respect as in any other case.
Heart-beating cadaver
Perfusion can be maintained mechanically (ventilator, medications). Allows procurement of multiple organs (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys, pancreas) that require oxygenated blood flow up until removal. Brain death must be confirmed.
Non-heart-beating cadaver
Restricted to organs that do NOT require continuous perfusion (primarily kidneys, sometimes liver and pancreas). Procurement must occur quickly after cardiac death to minimize ischemia time.
Controlled organ donation after death (DCD)
Planned withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment in a patient who does not meet brain death criteria; death is expected; procurement team is ready. A specific ethical and procedural protocol governs the timing and sequence of events.
Medical criteria
Not all patients are eligible for organ donation. Medical criteria assess each organ system independently. Age, medical history, cause of death, and organ function all factor into suitability. HIV-positive donors may now donate to HIV-positive recipients (HOPE Act).
Ethical dilemmas in organ donation arise when the patient has not left a clear directive before death. The family may disagree with each other or with documented wishes. The OPO and ethics committee provide guidance. The ST's role is to support the process professionally and maintain strict confidentiality.
End of life — clinical definition: Clinicians pronounce a patient has entered the end-of-life phase when death is expected within days, weeks, or months. Marked by ineffectiveness or cessation of life-saving protocols; comforting care (palliative) is still provided. Death has had to be redefined as medical knowledge and technology have advanced (e.g., brain death, persistent vegetative state).
Patients with Special Needs in the Perioperative Environment
Patients with a mental health history
May have heightened anxiety, altered perceptions, or be on psychiatric medications that interact with anesthesia. Communicate clearly; avoid triggers; do not withhold medications without anesthesia team approval. Maintain a calm, structured environment. Treat with dignity — mental illness does not diminish a person's rights.
Disoriented patients
May be confused due to age, dementia, delirium, drugs, or acute illness. Reassure frequently; use simple language; reorient gently ("You are in the hospital; you are safe"). Never leave a disoriented patient unattended. Safety straps are essential.
Pediatric patients
Children have unique anatomical, physiological, and psychosocial needs. Fear of separation from parents is paramount. Allow parents in pre-op as long as possible. Use child-appropriate language and distraction. Pediatric dosing for medications, smaller instruments, and different positioning considerations apply.
Older patients (geriatric)
May have multiple comorbidities, polypharmacy, decreased hearing/vision, skin fragility, and reduced physiologic reserve. Speak clearly and slowly; ensure hearing aids are in place pre-op; use extra padding for positioning; extra warming needed; longer recovery from anesthesia expected.
Patients with developmental disabilities
May have difficulty communicating pain, fear, or understanding instructions. Enlist family/caregivers for communication strategies. Be patient; allow more time for pre-op prep. Use visual cues and consistent routines. Maintain dignity — never speak about the patient as if they are not present.
Cultural Responses to Death and Dying
Cultural competence in this context is acquired through experience and active learning. It begins with acceptance and respect for all cultural perspectives on death.

Key considerations:
✦ Some cultures view death as a natural phenomenon — not feared but accepted
✦ May have special rituals for preparing the body for family viewing
✦ Specific religious requirements about who handles the body
✦ Dietary restrictions or spiritual items needed near the patient
The ST's approach:
✦ All postmortem preparation is carried out with dignity and respect
✦ Religious/cultural care preferences must be documented and followed
✦ Consult chaplaincy services for specific guidance
✦ Never assume — ask respectfully about needs and preferences

Cultural competence is NOT:
✦ Making assumptions based on race or ethnicity
✦ Treating all members of a group identically
✦ A fixed skill — it requires ongoing learning
